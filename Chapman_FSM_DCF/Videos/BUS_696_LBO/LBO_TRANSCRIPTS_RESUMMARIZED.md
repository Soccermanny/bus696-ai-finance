## 1. LBO-Modeling-Adding-a-DCF-Analysis-to-the-LBO-Model

The LBO model already contains all the infrastructure needed to run a discounted cash flow analysis since it is a full three-statement model. To attach the DCF, free cash flow to the firm (FCFF) is pulled directly from the LBO income statement by taking EBIT, tax-effecting it, adding back D&A and other non-cash charges, and subtracting capex and changes in working capital. The discount rate used is WACC, which can be computed from the post-LBO capital structure. Two valuation approaches are offered: the perpetuity growth method (terminal value = final-year FCFF × (1+g) / (WACC – g)), which for BMC implied a share value of approximately $50.58, and the exit-multiple method (terminal value = final-year EBITDA × exit multiple), which implied approximately $32 per share. The significant difference between the two methods illustrates how sensitive LBO-attached DCFs are to terminal value assumptions, and practitioners typically present both as a range. This dual-approach output is woven into the model's exit analysis section so that changing WACC or the growth rate immediately recalculates implied share value, providing a continuous reality check against the market offer price.

## 2. LBO-Modeling-Adjusting-the-Balance-Sheet-to-Reflect-the-LBO

After the sources and uses of funds are set, the pre-deal balance sheet must be adjusted to create a pro forma balance sheet that reflects the new capital structure of the LBO entity. The mechanics involve eliminating the old equity and legacy debt, recording new debt tranches at face value, and writing up assets if applicable, with goodwill absorbing the excess of purchase price over fair value of net assets acquired. Cash on the balance sheet is reduced by the amount used as a source of funds (e.g., BMC's $1.4B of the $1.58B in available cash), and capitalized financing fees are recorded as an asset (pre-2015) or as a contra-debt account (post-2015). The balance check — total assets equal total liabilities plus equity — is the key validation gate; if the model doesn't balance at the pro forma date, there is a misallocated debit or credit somewhere in the deal-related adjustments. Transaction fees hit retained earnings immediately as an expense, whereas financing fees are amortized over the life of the associated debt tranche. The fully adjusted pro forma balance sheet serves as the starting point for the five-year LBO forecast.

## 4. LBO-Modeling-Alarm-Over-Dividend-Recaps

Dividend recapitalizations have generated significant controversy because they transfer wealth from the leveraged company's creditors to equity sponsors without any corresponding operational improvement. The concern is that sponsors increase the debt load — and therefore default risk — of a portfolio company purely to monetize their equity position early, leaving lenders more exposed than when they originally committed capital. The BMC case is a prime example: seven months after the original LBO closed at roughly 75% debt-to-total-cap, Bain and Golden Gate added another $750 million in junior bonds, pushing equity as a percent of total capitalization down to approximately 7%, prompting Moody's to downgrade BMC's credit rating. Empirically, the data shows that companies that have executed dividend recaps default at higher rates than those that have not, which is the core of the alarm from the credit community. From the sponsor's perspective, the recap is rational because it reduces their basis in the investment and effectively converts some equity return into debt-like certainty of cash. The video contextualizes this tension as a fundamental agency problem between equity sponsors (who seek to maximize and accelerate returns) and debt holders (who bear the downside of increased leverage without receiving higher yields on their existing fixed-rate instruments).

## 5. LBO-Modeling-Asset-Sale-338Stock-Sale-Toggle-and-DTLs

In an LBO the transaction can be structured as either a stock sale or an asset sale (or a 338(h)(10) election), and the choice has material tax and accounting consequences. In a stock sale, the buyer acquires the legal entity and cannot step up asset values for tax purposes; consequently, the buyer writes assets up to fair market value for GAAP book purposes only, creating a deferred tax liability (DTL) equal to the write-up amount multiplied by the tax rate. This DTL reverses over the useful life of the written-up assets as the incremental depreciation/amortization flows through the income statement, representing future tax payments the company will eventually owe because its tax depreciation is lower than its book depreciation. In an asset sale or 338 election, the buyer obtains a tax basis step-up equal to the purchase price, eliminating the DTL but often resulting in a higher transaction price to compensate the seller for the taxable gain triggered. The LBO model includes a toggle cell (Stock Sale vs. Asset Sale) that switches the DTL calculation on or off and flows the change through to the pro forma balance sheet. Understanding this distinction is critical when comparing bids from different structures, as a tax step-up can be worth hundreds of millions of dollars in present-value tax savings to the buyer.

## 6. LBO-Modeling-Bonds

Bonds — specifically high-yield senior notes and subordinated notes — represent the unsecured or second-lien layer of LBO capital structures, sitting below bank term loans but above equity. Unlike leveraged loans, bonds are publicly registered (or Rule 144A), carry fixed coupons, and are typically issued at par with a bullet maturity (no scheduled principal amortization). Call protection is a defining feature: bond indentures usually prohibit early repayment for the first two to three years, after which the issuer can call at a premium that steps down toward par. In the BMC deal the senior notes were priced at 8.125% with an eight-year maturity; this fixed coupon creates a more predictable cost of debt than the floating-rate term loans but eliminates the ability to reduce debt cheaply during low-interest-rate periods. High-yield bonds carry ratings of BB or below (speculative grade), reflecting the elevated leverage; covenant packages for bonds tend to be incurrence-based (tested only when the company takes a specific action) rather than the maintenance covenants used for bank debt. In the LBO model the bond tranche requires a simplified amortization schedule (effectively zero mandatory amortization until maturity), and the interest expense is straightforward: coupon rate times the average outstanding balance, with no LIBOR floor complications.

## 7. LBO-Modeling-Capitalized-Financing-Fees

When a company borrows to fund an LBO, the fees paid to lenders (arrangement fees, upfront fees, original issue discounts) are treated differently from advisory and legal fees. Under pre-2015 GAAP, financing fees were recorded as a separate intangible asset on the balance sheet and amortized over the life of the borrowing; under the post-2015 rule, they are treated as a contra-debt account that reduces the carrying value of the debt. In both cases, the fees are amortized through the income statement as a component of interest expense — either separately labeled amortization or embedded in total interest — over the contractual term of the loan. Because this amortization is non-cash, it must be added back in the cash flow statement just like any other non-cash charge. In the BMC model, financing fees across all tranches total approximately $70 million; dividing by the respective terms (five years for the revolver, seven to eight years for term loans and bonds) yields annual amortization in the range of $10M. The post-2015 modeling modification requires removing the deferred financing fee line from intangible assets and instead netting it against total debt on the balance sheet, then ensuring that the annual amortization is wired into interest expense and added back in the cash flow statement.

## 9. LBO-Modeling-Cash

The treatment of the target company's existing cash balance is a nuanced element of LBO structuring. In the BMC deal, BMC had approximately $1.58 billion of cash on its balance sheet at deal close; the sponsors elected to use $1.4 billion of that as a source of funds (essentially reducing the amount of new capital that needed to be raised), retaining only a $180 million minimum cash balance as the floor for ongoing operations. This decision is captured in the "excess cash" line of the sources and uses table, and the minimum cash desired input drives both the revolver logic and the beginning-of-period cash balance in the pro forma balance sheet. A key model mechanic is the MAX function applied to excess cash — outputting the greater of zero or (existing cash minus minimum cash desired) — which prevents the model from showing negative cash as a source of funds if the minimum cash assumption is set aggressively. The minimum cash balance also defines the revolver trigger: if beginning-of-period cash plus pre-revolver cash flows falls below the minimum, the model automatically draws on the revolver. Structurally, leveraging the target's own cash balance to partially fund the buyout reduces the required sponsor equity contribution and therefore increases equity IRR, since the same exit proceeds are divided by a smaller initial investment.

## 10. LBO-Modeling-Completing-the-IS-and-CFS

With all debt tranches and interest expense schedules built, the income statement and cash flow statement can be fully completed. The key final additions to the income statement are: total interest expense (sum of all cash interest across all tranches), any non-cash pick interest expense that must pass through the income statement before being added back on the cash flow statement, and the amortization of deferred financing fees embedded in interest expense. ## 11. LBO-Modeling-Covenants

Loan covenants are contractual restrictions in credit agreements that protect lenders by limiting the borrower's financial risk-taking. Maintenance covenants require the borrower to maintain certain financial ratios (e.g., total debt/EBITDA ≤ 6×, interest coverage ≥ 2×) tested quarterly; a breach triggers default or waiver negotiations. Incurrence covenants, by contrast, are tested only when the company takes a specific action — such as paying a dividend, issuing more debt, or making an acquisition — and are considered much more borrower-friendly. The shift toward covenant-lite (cov-lite) structures has been dramatic: by 2018, approximately 75% of newly issued U.S. leveraged loans were cov-lite, meaning they contained only incurrence covenants for bank debt — an unprecedented level of lender permissiveness. This environment directly enables transactions like BMC's dividend recap, which triggered incurrence tests rather than ongoing maintenance covenants; had maintenance covenants been in place, the additional leverage from the recap might have caused a breach. Cov-lite benefits sponsors by reducing the frequency of lender oversight and default risk during operational downturns, but critics argue it expands moral hazard by allowing sponsors to load excessive debt without early tripwires. The model tracks compliance ratios in a summary table so analysts can quickly see how much covenant headroom exists under the base case and stress scenarios.

## 17. LBO-Modeling-Diluted-Shares-Part-1

Calculating diluted shares outstanding is an important prerequisite for computing the equity purchase price in a public company LBO. When a company is taken private, all outstanding stock options automatically vest upon the change of control; these options must be included in the diluted share count. The treasury stock method is used: for each option tranche, compute the number of in-the-money options, subtract the shares that could theoretically be repurchased with the option proceeds (at the offer price, not the strike), and the net is the incremental dilutive shares. Restricted stock units that have not yet vested are typically included at full face value because they will be paid out in the deal regardless of vesting status. The diluted share count for BMC at the $46.25 offer price was approximately 145.8 million shares, modestly above the basic count due to in-the-money options and unvested RSUs. The offer value (equity purchase price) equals diluted shares times offer price per share, which for BMC comes to approximately $6.7 billion — the primary "use of funds" in the sources and uses table.

## 19. LBO-Modeling-Distribution-Waterfall-Exercise-Part-1

Private equity fund distributions follow a strict waterfall that determines the order in which cash is returned to limited partners (LPs) and the general partner (GP). The first priority is return of capital: all committed LP capital contributed to fund investments must be returned before any profit-sharing begins. The second tier is the preferred return (hurdle rate), typically 8% per annum compounded, paid to LPs on their outstanding capital; this ensures that LPs earn at least a baseline return before the GP receives any carried interest. The third tier is the GP catch-up: once LPs have received their full capital plus 8% preferred return, the GP receives 100% of distributions (the "catch-up") until the GP has received approximately 20% of all profits above the hurdle. The fourth and final tier splits remaining distributions 80% to LPs and 20% to the GP as carried interest. The video works through a simplified numerical example with committed capital, multiple investment vintages, and realized proceeds to illustrate how the waterfall affects timing of GP compensation. The key takeaway is that the hurdle rate and catch-up provision mean GPs earn disproportionately large carried interest once returns exceed the hurdle, which powerfully aligns their incentives with generating above-hurdle returns.

## 20. LBO-Modeling-Distribution-Waterfall-Exercise-Part-2

## 26. LBO-Modeling-Forecasting-Accounts-Receivable

Accounts receivable is forecast as a percentage of revenue (days sales outstanding — DSO — converted to a decimal), using the historical average DSO as a base and then adjusting for any known changes in billing or collection policies. For BMC, historical DSO was approximately 70–80 days, which is reasonable for an enterprise software company where invoicing for multi-year contracts can create large upfront receivable balances. The roll-forward formula for AR is: beginning balance + revenue recognized − cash collected = ending balance; but in a percentage-of-revenue model, the ending balance equals (DSO/365) × annual revenue, and cash collected is back-solved as the plug. In the working capital section of the LBO cash flow statement, the change in AR is a cash flow driver: an increase in AR is an operating cash use (cash didn't come in yet), and a decrease is a source. It is important to model AR at the pro-forma level, not the GAAP level, because the LBO creates a new cost basis and any deferred revenue adjustments on the balance sheet affect the effective DSO. BMC's finance receivables (long-term installment receivables from software license arrangements) are modeled separately from trade AR because they have a different collection cycle and different balance sheet treatment.

## 27. LBO-Modeling-Forecasting-Intangibles-Goodwill-Other-Assets-Other-Liabilities

Intangible assets and goodwill are modeled differently from operating working capital items because they do not follow revenue-proportional patterns. Goodwill is created at the deal date (excess of purchase price over fair market value of net assets) and then remains constant unless there is an impairment; in the LBO model, goodwill is held flat in the five-year forecast because impairment testing is assumed not to trigger under the base case scenario. Acquired intangibles (customer lists, patents, trade names) are amortized over their useful lives using a straight-line method; the ending balance each period equals beginning balance minus amortization. For BMC, intangible assets include capitalized software development costs ($271M) and acquired intangibles ($190M), each with different useful life assumptions (roughly 3–5 years for software development costs). Other long-term assets and other long-term liabilities are typically held constant or rolled forward as percentages of revenue, depending on their nature; if there is no clear operational driver, holding them flat is the more conservative modeling choice. The video emphasizes that while these line items may seem immaterial individually, getting them right is essential for the balance sheet to balance after the five-year forecast.

## 28. LBO-Modeling-Forecasting-Other-Working-Capital-Items

Beyond accounts receivable, key working capital items include deferred revenue, accounts payable, accrued liabilities, and other current assets. Deferred revenue is a particularly important line for BMC because enterprise software companies collect cash upfront for multi-year maintenance and support contracts but recognize revenue ratably; deferred revenue is modeled as a percentage of bookings or as a percentage of total subscription revenue. Accounts payable is modeled as days payable outstanding (DPO) times COGS plus operating expenses, representing the average time the company takes to pay its vendors. Accrued liabilities (compensation accruals, rent, warranty reserves) are modeled as a fixed percentage of operating expenses or revenue. The net working capital definition used in the LBO model is: (AR + other current assets) − (AP + accrued liabilities + deferred revenue + other current liabilities), which for BMC was consistently negative (meaning the business is essentially self-funding from a working capital perspective because deferred revenue is large relative to receivables). A negative NWC position improves free cash flow because it means the company is collecting cash from customers before it has to pay its own expenses, a favorable dynamic highlighted in the LBO underwriting.

## 29. LBO-Modeling-Forecasting-PFBS-Assets

## 30. LBO-Modeling-Forecasting-PFBS-Liabilities-Equity

On the liability and equity side of the pro-forma balance sheet, the key drivers are: total debt outstanding (from the debt schedule roll-forwards minus any cash sweeps), accounts payable and accrued liabilities (from working capital), deferred revenue (from the revenue model), and the debt-contra account for capitalized financing fees (post-2015 accounting). The equity section is the most important for balance-sheet integrity: retained earnings roll forward as the cumulative sum of net income minus dividends; common stock and additional paid-in capital remain constant; and any stock-based compensation that does not flow through net income (e.g., stock option exercises) must be captured separately. In the LBO context, goodwill creation at the deal date effectively inflates the equity starting balance in the pro-forma; this "goodwill plug" is what makes the balance sheet balance at deal close despite the massive debt load. Each year's change in retained earnings equals net income minus any preferred dividends paid in cash (not PIK), and because the LBO entity is highly levered with large interest expense, net income in early years is often very small or negative, which keeps equity thin. The balance check is computed as: total assets − (total liabilities + total equity) = 0; any non-zero value indicates an error somewhere in the model linkage.

## 31. LBO-Modeling-Forecasting-PPE

Property, plant, and equipment (PP&E) for BMC is a relatively small line item (~$85M) because the company is software-based and not capital-intensive. The PP&E roll-forward is: beginning balance + capital expenditures − depreciation = ending balance. Historical data shows BMC's annual capex is ~$24M while annual depreciation is ~$38M, meaning the net PP&E balance is declining year over year — which is sustainable only if the company is not growing its fixed asset base but is rather managing an aging asset pool. In the forecast, capex is modeled as approximately 1% of revenue (consistent with historical levels), but an important check is added: the model tests whether projected depreciation would cause PP&E to go negative (which is physically impossible) and flags a warning. For the LBO model's EBITDA calculation, only standalone depreciation (not amortization) is relevant in the D&A reconciliation, so PP&E depreciation must be broken out from total D&A. A smoothing function is implemented for D&A forecasting: rather than projecting depreciation as a raw percentage of prior-year PP&E, the model uses a multi-period average to avoid artificial spikes caused by lumpy capex in any single year. This smoothed D&A estimate flows directly into both the income statement (reducing EBIT) and the cash flow statement (as a non-cash add-back), ensuring consistent EBITDA-to-cash-flow conversion.

## 32. LBO-Modeling-Forecasting-Software-Development-Costs

Software development costs for BMC (~$271M balance) follow accounting rules similar to intangible assets: new capitalized software investments increase the balance while amortization reduces it. The primary drivers are: (1) the annual capitalization rate, estimated as a percentage of total R&D spending based on historical patterns (typically 30–50% of total R&D is capitalized); (2) the useful life assumption (typically 3–5 years for software products), which drives the amortization rate. In the LBO model, amortization of capitalized software is broken out separately from PP&E depreciation because it is a distinct type of non-cash charge; however, both ultimately flow into total D&A for EBITDA calculation purposes. The key insight is that for a software company, R&D investment has a dual impact on the model: a portion goes directly to the income statement as current-period expense, while the capitalized portion sits on the balance sheet and amortizes over time, creating a deferred expense profile. In a growth scenario, if BMC increases R&D spending, both the current expense and the capitalized balance grow, which temporarily suppresses earnings but builds the intellectual property base. ## 37. LBO-Modeling-GP-vs-LP-Dynamics

The relationship between general partners (GPs) and limited partners (LPs) in a PE fund is governed by the limited partnership agreement (LPA), which defines each party's rights, obligations, and economics. LPs commit capital (the fund's equity base), bear the risk of loss, and receive the majority of returns; GPs manage the fund, make investment decisions, and receive management fees plus carried interest. The 2/20 structure (2% management fee on committed capital, 20% carry on returns above the hurdle) has compressed somewhat — many large funds now charge 1.5% or less in management fees — but carry percentages have remained at 20%. Information asymmetry is a core tension: GPs know far more about portfolio company performance than LPs, who typically receive quarterly reports with limited operational detail. LPs have responded by forming LP advisory committees, requiring enhanced reporting, and negotiating key-man provisions (which give LPs the right to exit the fund if the primary investment professional leaves). The GP-LP dynamic also affects deal structure: GPs sometimes co-invest alongside the fund on larger deals, which dilutes the LP's share of the upside but allows the GP to make larger bets while staying within fund concentration limits.

## 38. LBO-Modeling-How-PE-Funds-Work

A private equity fund is structured as a limited partnership, with the PE firm acting as the general partner and institutional investors (pension funds, insurance companies, endowments, sovereign wealth funds) as limited partners. The fund life is typically 10 years: five years of an investment period during which capital is deployed, followed by a five-year harvest period during which portfolio companies are exited and capital is returned to LPs. Within the fund, individual investments (portfolio companies) are owned through a series of holding companies and acquisition vehicles. The fund charges a management fee during the investment period (typically 1.5–2% of committed capital) and a reduced fee during the harvest period; this fee covers the GP's operating expenses and partner compensation before any carry is generated. Portfolio company returns are realized through three primary exit routes: IPO (listing on a public exchange), strategic sale (to a corporate acquirer), or secondary buyout (sale to another PE fund). The video provides historical data on the composition of LP bases: pension funds represent approximately 43% of PE LP capital, insurance companies and endowments approximately 48%, and sovereign wealth funds approximately 10%, with each having different return requirements and liquidity constraints that PE fund managers must understand.

## 39. LBO-Modeling-Impact-on-the-Balance-Sheet

The LBO transaction creates a pro-forma balance sheet that looks dramatically different from the pre-deal balance sheet. On the asset side: cash declines significantly (excess cash used as a source of funds), legacy assets remain but are written up if applicable, goodwill spikes (equal to purchase price premium over book value or fair value), and capitalized financing fees appear as a new asset. On the liability side: all legacy debt is retired and replaced with the new LBO debt structure (term loans, bonds), creating a much larger total debt balance. On the equity side: the pre-existing equity is completely wiped out and replaced by a small equity contribution from the sponsor and management, resulting in a dramatically lower equity book value despite the much higher enterprise value implied by the purchase price. The balance check is automatic — goodwill is the absorbing entry that ensures assets equal liabilities plus equity — which is why goodwill calculation must be the last accounting entry made in the pro-forma adjustment column. The video walks through each line of the adjustment column, confirming that debits equal credits and the pro-forma equity is negative in the recap structure (or a small positive in the LBO structure, absorbing goodwill).

## 40. LBO-Modeling-Impact-on-the-IS-and-CFS

The LBO transaction affects the ongoing income statement and cash flow statement in two principal ways: (1) massive new interest expense from the leveraged debt structure, and (2) D&A step-up from any asset write-ups or capitalized financing fee amortization. For BMC, the total annual interest expense increases from approximately $55M pre-deal to approximately $280–310M post-deal, reflecting the heavy debt load. This transforms BMC from a modestly profitable GAAP entity (pre-deal net income ~$200M+) into one that is near breakeven or slightly loss-making on a GAAP basis in early LBO years. The key insight is that the interest tax shield is maximized in this structure: because interest expense is the largest single deduction, the company pays very little in income taxes, effectively redirecting to debt service the cash that otherwise would have gone to the IRS. On the cash flow statement, the key change is the appearance of large financing cash flows: required debt principal payments, discretionary cash sweeps, and any revolver borrowings/repayments that did not exist pre-deal. The operating cash flow section changes only modestly from the pre-deal entity (reflecting the same operating business), confirming that the LBO's financial engineering works precisely because the underlying business generates strong operating cash flows.

## 41. LBO-Modeling-Initial-Valuation-Offer-Price-vs-Enterprise-Value

The LBO model supports two approaches to establishing the initial entry valuation: Approach 1 uses an assumed EV/EBITDA multiple (e.g., 7.3×) applied to LTM EBITDA to derive enterprise value, then backs into equity value by subtracting net debt; Approach 2 uses the explicit offer price per share (e.g., $46.25 for BMC), multiplied by diluted shares outstanding to get equity value, then adds net debt to get enterprise value. For the BMC case study, Approach 2 is the relevant one because the actual offer price was publicly disclosed in the merger announcement. The enterprise value under Approach 2 is calculated as: offer price × diluted shares + legacy debt − cash = enterprise value; for BMC this yields approximately $6.7B, implying a 7.3× multiple on ~$880M EBITDA. The choose function in the model allows the user to toggle between Approach 1 and Approach 2, with the selected approach feeding through the entire model (sources and uses, IRR calculations, exit valuation context). The premium/discount to the unaffected stock price ($45.42 prior to the announcement) was approximately 2% for BMC — an unusually low premium for a take-private, which later became a point of controversy among public shareholders.

## 42. LBO-Modeling-Interest-Income-and-Circularity

Interest income is earned on the company's cash balance and must be included in the income statement, but its calculation creates a circularity: interest income depends on the ending cash balance, the ending cash balance depends on net income, and net income depends on interest income. The standard approach to handling this is to use the average of the beginning-of-period and end-of-period cash balances as the base for interest income calculation, which is more accurate than using only the beginning balance (which would understate income in growing cash years) or only the ending balance (which creates a direct circular reference). In an LBO context there is a special consideration: the beginning-of-period cash balance in year 1 is the post-deal cash balance ($180M for BMC), which is dramatically lower than the pre-deal cash balance ($1.58B). Using the average of the pre-deal and post-deal balances would massively overstate year-1 interest income; using only the post-deal (pro-forma) balance as the beginning of period solves this. A circuit breaker toggle is implemented throughout the model to handle the remaining circularities from the revolver and term loan interest calculations: when turned "on," all circular cells are set to zero, breaking the loop and allowing the model to recalculate without a div/zero error. The circuit breaker pattern is industry-standard and appears in all professional-quality three-statement models.

## 43. LBO-Modeling-Introduction-to-LBOs

A leveraged buyout is the acquisition of a company using a significant amount of borrowed money — typically 70–80% of the purchase price — with the acquired company's own assets and cash flows used as collateral and repayment source. The financial sponsor (private equity firm) contributes the equity portion (20–30%), and the leveraged capital structure amplifies equity returns because a given improvement in EBITDA translates into a larger percentage gain on the small equity base. The house analogy is instructive: buying a $1M house with $200K of your own money and an $800K mortgage means a 10% appreciation in the house value doubles your equity, but a 10% decline wipes out half of it — leverage cuts both ways. Post-acquisition, the LBO company is private, which removes public market scrutiny and allows management to make longer-horizon decisions without quarterly earnings pressure. LBO firms typically hold portfolio companies for three to seven years before exiting through IPO, strategic sale, or secondary buyout. The three primary return drivers are: (1) EBITDA growth (operational value creation), (2) multiple expansion (selling at a higher EV/EBITDA multiple than the acquisition multiple), and (3) leverage — the debt paydown from operating cash flows that increases equity value.

## 44. LBO-Modeling-IRR-Sensitivity-to-Leverage-and-Valuation

This video uses data tables to show how the equity IRR changes as a function of two critical assumptions: the entry EV/EBITDA multiple and the leverage quantum (total debt-to-EBITDA). The output is a two-dimensional sensitivity table: one axis shows entry multiples from 6× to 9×, and the other shows leverage from 4× to 8× debt/EBITDA. The key insight is that leverage and IRR are positively correlated up to a point — more debt means less equity invested, which means a larger return on that equity — but the relationship is nonlinear and eventually counterproductive at extreme leverage levels where the added interest burden reduces cash available for debt repayment and increases default risk. Entry multiple and IRR are negatively correlated: overpaying (high entry multiple) compresses returns, while finding a cheap asset (low entry multiple) supercharges returns. The "sweet spot" in the current market environment (10–11× multiples) makes generating 20%+ IRRs extremely difficult without meaningful EBITDA growth or operational improvement, as the table visually confirms. This exercise is used by investment committees to set maximum bid prices: if the required return is 20%, the table shows the maximum entry multiple at each leverage level that achieves that hurdle.

## 45. LBO-Modeling-IRR-Sensitivity-to-Mezz-Debt-Terms

Mezzanine debt (subordinated notes, PIK instruments) is more expensive than senior debt (typically 12–20% all-in yield vs. 5–7% for term loans) but provides the financial sponsor with additional leverage beyond what senior lenders will provide. This video models how changing the mezz coupon rate, pick component, and warrant coverage affects both the mezz lenders' IRR and the equity sponsors' IRR. Higher mezz coupon rates increase interest expense and reduce free cash flow available for equity, lowering the equity IRR. However, higher mezz leverage also reduces the equity contribution (the denominator in the IRR calculation), which can offset the higher interest cost and still improve equity returns in strong exit scenarios. Warrant coverage — the percentage of equity given to mezz lenders as kickers — creates further dilution that reduces the equity sponsors' share of exit proceeds. The sensitivity table shows that the optimal mezz structure from an equity sponsor's perspective balances cost of capital (minimizing interest) against dilution (minimizing warrant coverage) to maximize after-dilution equity IRR. In today's market environment, true mezzanine financing has largely been replaced by high-yield second-lien bonds with equity kickers becoming rarer, reflecting the compressed return expectations in the credit markets.

## 46. LBO-Modeling-LBO-Capital-Structure-Debt

The debt side of an LBO capital structure is layered in order of seniority, security, and cost. At the top (most senior, cheapest, highest security): the revolving credit facility and Term Loan A (pro-rata bank debt), secured by a first lien on substantially all assets, typically priced at LIBOR plus 300–500 basis points. Next is Term Loan B, institutional leveraged loans sold to CLOs, mutual funds, and hedge funds; also first-lien secured but with looser covenants and longer terms (7 years vs. 5 years for TLA). Senior notes (high-yield bonds) are unsecured or second-lien, fixed-rate instruments with bullet maturities; they sit below the term loans in the priority waterfall but above subordinated and junior notes. Subordinated or "mezzanine" notes are unsecured, often with PIK toggle options and equity warrants, representing the highest-cost debt in the structure. Preferred equity is technically equity but behaves like debt: fixed dividends, seniority over common equity, and PIK accrual options. The total debt quantum is expressed as a multiple of LTM EBITDA, with each tranche contributing a specified number of "turns." For BMC: revolver (0×), TLA (3.3×), TLB (0.75×), senior notes (1.85×), totaling approximately 5.9× total debt/EBITDA.

## 47. LBO-Modeling-LBO-Capital-Structure-Equity

The equity side of an LBO capital structure is smaller than in a typical acquisition because the heavy debt load amplifies returns. Sponsor equity (from the PE firm's fund) represents the residual: total uses of funds minus all debt proceeds minus excess cash used from the balance sheet. Management rollover is equity that existing management contributes by converting their pre-existing shares into shares of the new LBO entity rather than receiving cash; this reduces the sponsor's required cash contribution and aligns management with the same exit outcome as the sponsor. In the BMC deal, Bain Capital and Golden Gate contributed approximately $1.25 billion in equity, with management rolling approximately $176 million, for a total equity base of ~$1.4 billion against total capitalization of ~$8.3 billion (~17% equity). Equity holders receive value only after all debt, preferred, and mezzanine tranches are satisfied at exit — the "residual claimant" position — which means their upside is unlimited but their downside is capped at their initial contribution. The management equity pool is structured to provide significant upside: management effectively owns 14.6% of equity, which on a total equity value of $6B translates to nearly $900M of value, versus a rollover contribution of $176M, generating an implied 5× cash-on-cash return.

## 50. LBO-Modeling-Mezzanine-Financing-Bridge-Loans

Mezzanine financing occupies the space between senior secured debt and equity in the capital structure, providing capital that senior lenders will not but at a higher cost than senior debt. Classic mezzanine is structured as subordinated notes with a cash coupon (8–14%), a PIK component (2–4%), and equity warrants (1–5% of fully diluted equity), resulting in all-in yields of 15–20%. Bridge loans are short-term (6–18 month) financing instruments used when permanent financing hasn't been arranged in time for deal close; they "bridge" the company to a permanent high-yield bond issuance or other long-term facility. In the BMC LBO, the initial $1.7B bridge loan was replaced by the permanent $1.625B high-yield bond at 8.125% after the deal closed. Bridge loans typically carry a step-up provision: the interest rate increases by 50–100 basis points every few months until the bridge is converted to permanent financing, incentivizing the borrower to execute the permanent deal quickly. True mezzanine (subordinated notes with warrants) has declined as an LBO financing instrument in recent years because the rise of cov-lite leveraged loans and second-lien term loans has provided similar subordinated leverage at lower cost, without equity dilution from warrants.

## 53. LBO-Modeling-Modeling-Asset-Write-Ups

When an LBO is structured as an acquisition (as opposed to a leveraged recapitalization) for accounting purposes, GAAP requires that any assets with fair market values exceeding their book values be written up to fair value, with the residual purchase price premium recorded as goodwill. The simplified approach used earlier in the model computed goodwill as purchase price minus book value of equity; the more rigorous approach introduced in this video starts with book equity, adds the fair market value write-ups for PP&E ($300M assumed) and intangible assets ($200M assumed) with useful lives of 10 and 15 years respectively, and then computes goodwill as the residual. The write-ups increase the beginning balance of PP&E and intangibles on the pro-forma balance sheet, which then generates incremental depreciation and amortization over the write-up period — further reducing taxable income in a stock sale context. A toggle cell determines whether the deal is structured as an LBO (acquisition) or a leveraged recap: if it is a recap, GAAP does not treat it as a change of control, write-ups are not recognized, and goodwill is zero (leaving the balance sheet with a potentially negative equity). The key insight is that regardless of how the purchase price is allocated among assets, the total equity value in the pro-forma balance sheet is always the same because goodwill absorbs the remaining premium.

## 54. LBO-Modeling-Modeling-Cash-Sweeps

The cash sweep is a standard feature of LBO models that allows the company to use excess cash (after required principal payments and revolver management) to make optional (discretionary) prepayments on term loans. The mechanics are: starting with pre-revolver cash flows, subtract any revolver paydown, and what remains is the cash available for the sweep. The model applies a MIN function to cap the sweep at the lower of (a) available cash or (b) the remaining term loan balance, preventing the balance from going negative. A separate "percent of available cash used" toggle allows the analyst to model scenarios where less than 100% of excess cash is swept — for example, if there are prepayment penalties or if the company wants to retain more cash. The sweep cascade follows the priority waterfall: Term Loan A gets first priority for the sweep (since it is most senior and its lenders have the highest protection rights), then Term Loan B gets what remains after TLA is exhausted. Senior notes and subordinated notes typically do not receive sweeps because bonds have call protection provisions that make prepayment economically costly. The cash sweep mechanic is central to the LBO return story: the faster debt is paid down, the larger the equity portion of enterprise value at exit, improving equity returns.

## 55. LBO-Modeling-Modeling-Hurdle-Rate-Sensitivity

This video reframes the typical LBO analysis by asking the inverse question: rather than "given the purchase price, what is my IRR?", the hurdle rate sensitivity asks "given my required minimum IRR, what is the maximum price I am willing to pay?" Using Excel's PV function, the maximum equity investment (from the sponsor's perspective) is calculated as the present value of the exit equity proceeds discounted at the hurdle rate. This maximum equity contribution, combined with all the other capital sources (debt tranches, excess cash), yields the maximum offer value. For a 15% hurdle rate, the analysis shows the sponsor could have paid up to $58.43/share (vs. the actual $46.25) and still achieved 15% returns — implying significantly more premium could have been offered. The resulting table maps out maximum offer price per share and maximum EV/EBITDA multiple at hurdle rates ranging from 15% to 25%; at higher hurdle rates, the maximum offer price declines. This sensitivity is a key deal-screening tool: investment committees use it to set bid limits that preserve the required return threshold across various leverage and exit scenarios. The video emphasizes the importance of anchoring exit equity value correctly — using only the sponsor's portion rather than total equity when management or mezzanine holders participate.

## 56. LBO-Modeling-Modeling-Interest-Expenses

Interest expense for each debt tranche is calculated differently based on the instrument type: floating-rate bank loans (revolver, TLA, TLB) use LIBOR plus a spread; fixed-rate bonds use a stated coupon. For LIBOR-based instruments, a MAX function enforces the LIBOR floor: the effective LIBOR used in the calculation is the greater of the actual LIBOR rate and the floor (e.g., 1% for TLA, 0% for TLB and revolver). In more sophisticated models, the forward LIBOR curve (available from Bloomberg) can be used to project LIBOR across the forecast period, reflecting market expectations; a simpler approach is to hold current LIBOR constant. Interest income from the cash balance is calculated using the average beginning-of-period and end-of-period cash balances to correctly reflect the post-deal lower starting balance in year 1. The revolving credit line, TLA, and TLB interest expense formulas all create circularity because the end-of-period balances depend on cash sweeps, which depend on net income, which depends on interest expense; a circuit breaker toggle resolves this by zeroing out the circular cells when needed. Senior notes and subordinated notes do not create the same circularity because their balances are not affected by discretionary cash sweeps. The massive interest burden visible in the completed income statement — where interest expense is often 30–40% of EBITDA — is a defining characteristic of the LBO income statement and is the primary mechanism for the interest tax shield.

## 58. LBO-Modeling-Modeling-LBO-Goodwill-and-a-Recap-Toggle

Goodwill in an LBO is computed as the excess of the purchase price (offer value) over the fair market value of the net assets acquired, accounting for any PP&E and intangible write-ups. For BMC at $6.7B purchase price against $818M book equity and $500M in write-ups, goodwill is approximately $5.4B under the more rigorous approach (vs. $5.9B under the simplified approach without write-ups). The goodwill entry is an asset that, combined with the deal-related liabilities and new equity, ensures the pro-forma balance sheet balances with zero residual. The LBO vs. recap toggle introduces a model feature where switching to "recap" mode (a leveraged recapitalization rather than a change-of-control acquisition) sets goodwill to zero and write-ups to zero, since FASB/IFRS only requires purchase price allocation in a genuine change-of-control transaction. In recap mode, the pro-forma equity becomes deeply negative (offer value minus book equity, with no goodwill to absorb the difference) — this is technically correct from an accounting standpoint but requires explanation to stakeholders who may not understand why equity is negative. The key practical insight is that the toggle has no impact on returns — IRRs, cash flows, and EBITDA are identical regardless of whether goodwill is recognized — because goodwill is purely an accounting entry that does not affect cash.

## 59. LBO-Modeling-Modeling-PIK-Debt-Preferreds

PIK (paid-in-kind) interest is a financing feature in which the borrower, rather than paying cash interest, allows the interest to accrue to the principal balance of the debt, increasing the amount owed but not requiring current cash outflow. Subordinated notes in LBOs sometimes have a PIK component alongside a cash component (e.g., 8% cash + 4% PIK, total 12%); the PIK portion increases the outstanding principal, which compounds over time, significantly increasing the balloon repayment at maturity. Preferred stock has similar features: cash dividends are paid at a stated cash rate on the outstanding preferred balance, while PIK dividends accrue to principal (increasing the preferred balance). In the model, the PIK component is calculated as the PIK rate times the beginning-of-period balance (net of any mandatory amortization), and this amount is added to the ending balance of the instrument. The cash component does NOT increase principal but reduces available cash for debt service. BMC had neither subordinated notes nor preferred stock in its actual deal, but the model is built to handle these instruments for generality. The PIK toggle note in the dividend recap (a bond where the issuer can elect cash or PIK interest depending on cash levels) is a more sophisticated variant modeled in a separate video.

## 60. LBO-Modeling-Modeling-Required-Debt-Amortization

Term loans, unlike bonds, require scheduled principal repayments throughout their life rather than a single balloon at maturity. The mandatory amortization schedule is negotiated at deal close and expressed as a percentage of the original principal borrowed. For BMC's TLA at $2.88B, a 10% mandatory amortization in year 1 followed by 5% annually represents the assumed schedule (since BMC did not disclose the exact schedule post-privatization). The formula structure wraps a MIN function around the mandatory payment: MIN(amortization rate × original balance, beginning-of-period balance), ensuring the calculated paydown never exceeds the remaining balance and cannot drive the balance negative. Term Loan B at $670M similarly has a 5% assumed annual amortization (lower because TLBs typically have very light amortization requirements, often as low as 1% annually). Senior bonds have zero mandatory amortization (bullet maturity), and subordinated notes also typically have zero mandatory amortization. The required amortization appears as an operating cash outflow in the financing activities section of the cash flow statement, reducing free cash available for the revolver and discretionary sweeps. These mandatory payments are the primary mechanism by which debt is retired in years 1–2 of the LBO, before cash sweeps from operational cash generation become the dominant deleveraging source.

## 61. LBO-Modeling-Modeling-the-Revolver

The revolving credit facility is a flexible borrowing line that the company draws on when it needs cash and repays when it has excess cash, functioning like a business credit card. The revolver formula is built around the concept of "pre-revolver cash flows": operating cash flows net of investing activities and required debt principal payments, but explicitly excluding the revolver itself to avoid circularity. If pre-revolver cash flows are positive (surplus), the revolver is paid down (up to its outstanding balance); if negative (deficit), the revolver is drawn upon (up to its maximum availability of $350M for BMC). A MIN function governs the revolver balance, ensuring it never goes below zero (paydown is capped at the outstanding balance) and never exceeds the facility maximum ($350M). For BMC, the revolver starts at $0 drawn at deal close because the sponsors deliberately designed the capital structure so that operating cash flows would cover debt service without needing to draw the revolver. The revolver change in each period (borrow or repay) flows through the financing activities section of the cash flow statement. The video emphasizes that using the wrong "cash available" figure (e.g., using net income or total operating cash flows instead of the pre-revolver figure) is one of the most common and consequential errors in building the revolver formula.

## 62. LBO-Modeling-Modeling-the-Revolver-Borrowing-Base-Compliance

In addition to the nominal $350M availability cap, revolving credit lines are often subject to a borrowing base limitation: the maximum amount the company can borrow is capped at a percentage of the most liquid collateral assets — typically 80% of accounts receivable plus 60–65% of inventory. This mechanism protects lenders by ensuring that revolver borrowings are always backed by specific, liquid assets that can be liquidated in a default scenario. The compliance check is built in the model using a MIN function that caps availability at the lesser of (a) the nominal $350M or (b) 80% of AR plus 65% of other collateral assets, calculated at each period-end. For BMC, whose AR balance is large relative to its nominal facility size, the borrowing base does not bind under normal operating conditions — but in a stress scenario where revenue declines and AR contracts sharply, the borrowing base could tighten, reducing available liquidity precisely when the company needs it most. An error flag is built into the model: if the actual revolver drawn exceeds the maximum availability (including any borrowing base limitation), the cell displays an "OVERDRAWN" error, which can be surfaced on an error dashboard. This compliance check is a realistic modeling feature that differentiates a sophisticated LBO model from a simplified one.

## 63. LBO-Modeling-Post-2015-Financing-Fee-Rules-in-LBO-Models

In 2015, the Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB) changed the accounting treatment for debt issuance costs (financing fees), requiring them to be recorded as a contra-debt account (reducing the carrying value of the debt on the balance sheet) rather than as a separate intangible asset. Before this change, the $70M in BMC's financing fees would have been recorded as a deferred financing fee asset and amortized as a separate non-cash charge; under the new rule, the fees reduce the net debt carrying value from day one, and the amortization flows through as a component of interest expense rather than separately. The practical modeling difference is: (1) the intangible assets section no longer shows capitalized financing fees; (2) the debt balance is reduced by the unamortized contra-debt amount; and (3) interest expense includes the annual fee amortization as an embedded non-cash component that must be added back in the cash flow statement. The retained earnings section no longer requires a manual adjustment for the non-cash amortization because it now flows through the income statement correctly. The video notes that the new rule creates a minor discrepancy: technically the fees should be allocated tranche-by-tranche to their respective debt instruments, but for simplicity the model lumps all fees into a single contra-debt schedule against total debt, which has no impact on returns or cash flows.

## 64. LBO-Modeling-Preparing-the-PPE-Schedule

The PP&E historical schedule for BMC shows a consistent pattern of capex below depreciation ($24M capex vs. $38M depreciation in recent years), meaning net PP&E has been declining from $87.8M to $85.2M. This is unusual for a growing company and reflects BMC's cost-cutting posture as a mature enterprise software business that is underinvesting in physical infrastructure. Depreciation expense is found in BMC's annual report footnotes (note 4, property, plant and equipment), disclosed separately from the combined D&A figure on the cash flow statement at approximately $38–39M per year. The key forecast concern is that if the capex-below-depreciation pattern continues for all five forecast years, PP&E approaches zero and could theoretically go negative — which is impossible physically. The model therefore uses a smoothed D&A approach: D&A is projected as a function of the ending PP&E balance using historical depreciation-to-PPE ratios, and a floor is established so that depreciation cannot exceed the PP&E balance. This smoothing function prevents the nonsensical declining PP&E from flowing through to the EBITDA calculation as unrealistically low depreciation. The historical capex data (1% of revenue) is sourced directly from the cash flow statement's investing activities section and serves as the primary capex forecast driver.

## 65. LBO-Modeling-Preparing-the-Software-Development-Costs-Intangibles-and-Goodwill-Schedules

## 67. LBO-Modeling-Returns-to-Senior-Debt

Returns to senior debt providers (revolver, TLA, TLB, senior notes) are simpler to calculate than equity returns because they do not depend on the exit multiple — senior debt holders receive their principal and interest regardless of whether the company is sold for 6× or 10× EBITDA. The return schedule is built from the lender's perspective: initial outflow (capital lent on day 1), periodic inflows (interest payments, mandatory amortization, and cash sweeps during the holding period), and a terminal inflow (any residual principal outstanding at the end of year 5, which is assumed to be refinanced or repaid upon exit). For TLA with $2.88B initially, 5% annual amortization (~$144M/year), cash sweeps of ~$300M/year, and interest at ~5%: the IRR works out to approximately 5.2–5.5%, consistent with the LIBOR-plus-400bps pricing. For senior notes at 8.125% with zero amortization (bullet), the IRR equals the coupon rate of 8.125%, since principal is returned in full at maturity. IFERROR functions wrap all IRR formulas to avoid error display when the revolver is never drawn (no investment means division by zero in IRR). ## 68. LBO-Modeling-Returns-to-Sub-Debt

Subordinated note and preferred equity holders occupy a unique position: they have a debt-like base return (from the stated coupon or dividend plus PIK accruals) plus an equity upside (from warrants). For a hypothetical sub-note tranche of $220M at 12% (8% cash + 4% PIK) with 1% equity warrant coverage: during the holding period, periodic cash flows include cash interest only (the PIK accrues but is not received in cash); at exit, the note holder receives principal + all accrued PIK + final period cash interest + 1% of exit equity. At a 7.3× exit multiple, the equity kicker adds ~$61M (1% of $6.1B exit equity) to the note holder's proceeds. The IRR on this combined structure exceeds the cash coupon because the PIK compounding and equity upside add to the final period return; for BMC's hypothetical sub-note, the IRR at 7.3× exit is approximately 14–16%, growing to 18–20% at 7.8× exit. The formula for the exit period is: cash interest + mandatory amortization + PIK accruals (already captured in ending balance) + ending principal balance + equity kicker. Building this returns schedule confirms the intuition that subordinated creditors are compensated for their lower seniority with higher all-in returns, bridging the gap between senior debt (~5–8%) and equity (~33–38%).

## 72. LBO-Modeling-Tax-Reform-Impact-on-the-Structure-of-PE-Part-1

The 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act changed the corporate tax rate from 35% to 21% for C corporations and also reduced the effective tax rate for pass-through entities from 39.6% to a best-case 33.4% (for entities qualifying for the full 20% qualified business income deduction). Prior to tax reform, the pass-through structure was clearly superior for PE firms and their portfolio companies: a single level of tax at ~40% was far better than the C-corp double tax (35% corporate + 23.8% dividend tax = ~50.5% effective rate). After tax reform, the effective C-corp cumulative rate dropped to ~39.8%, much closer to the pass-through rate of 33.4–40.8%. Most PE funds remain organized as limited partnerships because (a) converting to C-corp is difficult to reverse if tax rates change again, (b) the conversion would eliminate carried interest (which is only available in partnership structures), and (c) single-level taxation is still slightly better for most PE fund structures in most scenarios. However, some large public PE firms (Blackstone, KKR, Apollo) have converted or considered converting to C-corp to unlock inclusion in stock indices and reduce complexity for public shareholders. For portfolio companies specifically, the decision is more nuanced and depends on whether the business generates qualified business income (QBI) that qualifies for the 20% deduction.

## 73. LBO-Modeling-Tax-Reform-Impact-on-the-Structure-of-PE-Part-2

Despite the narrowed gap between C-corp and pass-through tax rates, most PE firms are expected to stay organized as limited partnerships primarily to preserve carried interest. Carried interest — the 20% of profits above the hurdle rate that GPs receive from LPs — has historically been taxed at long-term capital gains rates (20%) rather than ordinary income rates (40%), representing a massive tax advantage for PE firm partners. The 2017 tax reform changed the qualifying holding period for long-term capital gains treatment from one year to three years, a modest tightening that does not significantly affect PE firms whose typical holding period is three to five years. However, the management fee waiver practice — where some GPs were converting management fees into carry to take advantage of the capital gains rate — was addressed, making it harder to lump fee income into carry. The 20% capital gains rate vs. 33.4% ordinary income rate creates a potential tax advantage of approximately 13 percentage points for a PE partner's compensation, which is large enough that the prospect of eliminating it (by converting to C-corp) is not attractive for most private PE firms. Add-on acquisitions present a new complication: GPs must structure bolt-on purchases carefully to avoid resetting the three-year holding period clock for the entire platform, which could subject some gains to ordinary income rates.

## 74. LBO-Modeling-Tax-Reform-Impact-Part-1

The 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act introduced a new limitation on interest expense deductibility for companies with over $25M in revenue: net interest expense cannot exceed 30% of EBITDA plus interest income (a measure called "adjusted taxable income" or ATI), at least until 2022; after 2022, the cap tightens to 30% of EBIT (excluding D&A). This directly impacts highly leveraged PE-backed companies because their interest expense routinely exceeds 30% of EBITDA. For a company with $880M EBITDA and $300M in interest expense, the deductible limit is $264M (30% × $880M), leaving $36M non-deductible; this effectively reduces the present-value tax shield from leverage. Two offsetting effects partially compensate: (1) the overall lower corporate tax rate (21% vs. 35%) reduces taxes paid on any given dollar of income, which improves post-tax cash flows; and (2) the 2017 law enhanced bonus depreciation provisions, allowing companies to immediately deduct 100% of the cost of qualifying assets placed in service (rather than depreciating over useful lives), dramatically reducing near-term taxable income and improving year-1 cash flows. Non-deductible interest can be carried forward indefinitely, so the limitation is a timing issue rather than a permanent loss of the shield, particularly as the company delivers over the holding period.

## 75. LBO-Modeling-Tax-Reform-Impact-Part-2

Taking the 2017 tax reform changes in totality, Hamilton Lane's research concluded that the net effect on PE returns was positive, estimated at a 3–17% improvement in PE-level returns despite the interest expense deductibility limits. The primary reasons are: the lower 21% corporate tax rate improves after-tax operating cash flows, bonus depreciation allows accelerated tax shields in the early years when depreciation exceeds deductible interest anyway, and the interest expense carryforward means the lost shield is recovered when the company delvers rather than permanently forgone. Portfolio companies structured as pass-throughs benefit from the QBI deduction and do not face the double-taxation risk of C-corp status, maintaining the single-tax advantage. For the private equity model specifically, the reduction in ordinary income rates for pass-through entities (from 39.6% to 33.4% best case) reduces the tax benefit of debt to a degree, since the "tax shield" is now worth less per dollar of deductible interest. However, the consensus view is that the industry adapted to the post-reform environment and that returns have not materially deteriorated from the tax reform alone, though the simultaneous compression from high entry multiples and elevated dry powder is a more significant headwind.

## 76. LBO-Modeling-Tax-Savings-in-LBOs

The tax shield of debt — the reduction in taxes paid due to the deductibility of interest expense — is one of the foundational value-creation mechanisms in LBOs. At a 25% tax rate, each dollar of interest expense saves $0.25 in taxes, making the effective after-tax cost of debt (interest rate × (1 − tax rate)) materially below the nominal rate: a 5% coupon bond effectively costs only 3.75% after-tax. This shield has historically been enhanced by three factors: high leverage (more debt = more interest), high tax rates (more to save), and high interest rates (more interest to deduct). Since the early 1980s when LBOs gained prominence, all three factors have declined: leverage is lower (60% vs. 80–90% in the 1980s), tax rates are lower (21% vs. 35%), and interest rates are lower. The result is that the tax shield is a less potent return driver today than it was in the LBO heyday of the 1980s. This reinforces the strategic importance of operational value creation — sponsors who can meaningfully grow EBITDA through pricing power, cost reduction, market expansion, or add-on acquisitions will generate compelling returns even without the turbo-charged tax shields of earlier cycles. The video frames this as one reason why modern PE has evolved from "financial engineering" to a more operationally-focused model.

## 77. LBO-Modeling-Term-Loans-the-Revolver-Part-1

Term loans (both bank-originated TLA and institutional TLB) and revolving credit facilities form the most senior layer of the LBO capital structure, typically secured by a first lien on substantially all of the company's assets. Key structural features of leveraged loans: floating rate pricing at SOFR (formerly LIBOR) plus a spread of 300–500bps; scheduled principal amortization (TLA: 15–20% per year, TLB: 1% per year); no call protection (can be repaid at any time without penalty); typical term of 5 years for TLA and 7 years for TLB. The institutional leveraged loan market (TLB) is dominated by CLOs (collateralized loan obligations), mutual funds, and hedge funds, which have replaced bank lenders as the primary providers of LBO credit. Examples from the video: Carlyle's Ortho Clinical LBO ($4.15B) used a $2.175B TLB at LIBOR+375bps plus 1% LIBOR floor and a $350M revolver; Blackstone's Gates LBO ($5.4B) used $2.49B plus €200M term loans and $125M cash flow revolver plus $325M asset-based revolver. Covenant-lite (cov-lite) structures, where bank debt covenants are eliminated or limited to incurrence tests rather than maintenance tests, became standard for institutional leveraged loans by 2018 — a profound shift that benefits borrowers (sponsors and portfolio companies) at the expense of lender protection.

## 78. LBO-Modeling-Term-Loans-the-Revolver-Part-2

Continuing the discussion of leveraged loan market dynamics, this video examines credit spreads and revolver mechanics. Over the 20-year period from ~2002 to the recording date, single-B spreads (typical LBO issuer rating) averaged LIBOR plus 400–600bps, spiking dramatically during the 2008–2010 financial crisis (to as high as LIBOR+2000bps) before reverting to near pre-crisis levels. The gradual decline in spreads reflects the accommodative credit cycle, high investor demand (from CLOs and other yield-seeking fixed-income investors), and the structural shift toward cov-lite (which benefits issuers). The revolver is typically packaged with the term loan tranche as a single syndicated deal sold to the same investor base; it is usually the same maturity as TLA (5 years) and carries similar pricing. The availability on the revolver can be either a flat dollar amount (e.g., $350M for BMC) or an asset-based lending formula (a percentage of AR and inventory). Dell's LBO included a $2B asset-based revolver of which $750M was drawn at close; Blackstone's Gates deal had both a $125M cash-flow revolver and a $325M asset-based revolver, showing that hybrid structures are common in larger deals. LIBOR is being phased out and replaced by SOFR (Secured Overnight Financing Rate) as the floating-rate benchmark for U.S. leveraged loans.

## 79. LBO-Modeling-The-Cash-Flow-Statement

The LBO cash flow statement is structured in three sections: operating, investing, and financing. Operating cash flows start with GAAP net income, add back non-cash items (D&A, SBC, PIK interest accruals), and adjust for changes in working capital (increases in NWC are uses of cash, decreases are sources). Investing cash flows are dominated by capital expenditures, purchases of software development costs (a major capitalized investing outflow for BMC), and acquisitions if any. Financing cash flows include: required debt principal payments (TLA, TLB amortization), revolver borrowings/repayments, cash sweeps (discretionary prepayments), preferred cash dividends, and any sponsor dividends (relevant only in the dividend recap scenario). The "pre-revolver cash flow" is a critical derived line: sum of operating + investing + required financing payments, but excluding revolver. This is the input to the revolver formula. BMC's base case generates approximately $800M–$1B in operating cash flows and ~$200M in capex, yielding free cash flows of ~$600–800M before debt service. After required amortization payments of ~$200–300M in early years, approximately $300–600M per year remains available for the cash sweep. The sanity check compares model-derived operating cash flows to management guidance; close alignment confirms the operational assumptions are reasonable before proceeding to the debt service layer.

## 80. LBO-Modeling-Understanding-BMCs-Dividend-Recap

In April 2014, just seven to eight months after the BMC LBO closed, Bain Capital and Golden Gate issued a $750M subordinated PIK toggle bond and paid themselves the proceeds as a dividend — recovering ~60% of their original $1.25B equity contribution. The bond was priced at a 9% coupon (cash or PIK toggle) with a 2019 maturity. This transaction was controversial because: (1) Moody's immediately downgraded BMC's credit rating, noting that equity as a percent of total capitalization fell from ~18% to ~7%; (2) the company was already facing operating challenges with Moody's estimating a 4.5% revenue decline in 2013 versus management's original growth projections; and (3) BMC was able to complete the transaction only because the credit markets were extremely accommodating, allowing a highly levered company to issue yet more debt. The PIK toggle feature was specifically designed to provide cash flow flexibility: if BMC's cash fell below a specified threshold, the company could elect to pay interest in the form of additional principal rather than cash, avoiding a potential default triggered by insufficient liquidity. From the sponsor's perspective, the recap reduces their effective basis from $1.25B to ~$500M while still owning 100% of the equity, dramatically improving the potential IRR if the company's ultimate exit value is maintained.

## 81. LBO-Modeling-What-Makes-a-Good-LBO

Financial sponsors look for specific characteristics in LBO candidates that make the leverage-heavy structure viable and the return potential compelling. Predictable, non-cyclical cash flows are the most important quality: if revenue and EBITDA are steady and relatively immune to economic cycles, the company can service large fixed debt payments even in downturns. Low ongoing capital expenditure requirements maximize free cash flow for debt paydown; capital-intensive businesses (manufacturing, utilities) are harder to lever up profitably. A suboptimal (underleveraged) capital structure represents an arbitrage opportunity: a company that could support more debt but hasn't leveraged up is leaving tax shields on the table that a PE sponsor can capture by recapitalizing the business. Undervaluation — trading at a low EV/EBITDA multiple due to industry cyclicality, mismanagement, or market neglect — creates multiple expansion potential at exit. Strong management teams that are being constrained by public market short-termism are ideal: they will welcome the private ownership structure and have greater alignment through equity incentives. Finally, divisionalized businesses with separable non-core subsidiaries offer an immediate post-close deleveraging option — selling a division can retire a significant portion of the LBO debt in the first 12 months, rapidly improving the risk profile of the remaining business.

## 82. LBO-Modeling-What-the-KKR-Deal-Tells-Us-About-Likely-Actual-IRRs

In 2018, KKR acquired BMC from Bain Capital and Golden Gate in a secondary PE buyout, providing an opportunity to reverse-engineer the actual returns earned by the original LBO sponsors. KKR's deal terms were disclosed: $1.85B in bonds, $4.35B in term loans, $300M in preferred equity, and $2B in KKR equity, implying a total transaction value of approximately $8.5B. Using a conservative assumption that BMC paid down zero of its LBO debt between 2013 and 2018 (no EBITDA-generated deleveraging), the implied equity value received by Bain/Golden Gate from KKR is approximately $8.5B minus the ~$6B in outstanding debt = ~$2.4B. Against the net equity basis of ~$500M (initial $1.25B minus the $750M dividend recap proceeds), the implied IRR using an XIRR function with actual dates (September 2013 LBO close, April 2014 dividend, June 2018 KKR sale) is approximately 29–33%. Remarkably, this is essentially identical to the 33% IRR that the course's LBO model predicted under the base-case assumption of a 7.3× exit multiple — confirming that the model's assumptions were well-calibrated. If Bain/Golden Gate actually paid down some debt (which they likely did given BMC's cash generation), the actual IRR would be higher, potentially 41–55% depending on the paydown percentage.

## 83. LBO-Modeling-Whole-Fund-vs-Deal-by-Deal-Distribution-Model

When private equity funds distribute proceeds from portfolio company exits, carry is calculated under one of two models: whole-fund (also called "total return" or "all-in" waterfall) or deal-by-deal. In the whole-fund model, the GP does not receive any carried interest until all LP committed capital is returned plus the 8% preferred return across the entire fund portfolio; this protects LPs against GPs taking carry on early winners while later investments perform poorly. In the deal-by-deal model, carry is computed on each investment's realized proceeds in isolation; GPs receive carry faster when early deals perform well, even if later deals ultimately drag total fund performance below the hurdle. Deal-by-deal benefits GPs in terms of cash flow timing (they get paid sooner) and is more common in U.S. fund structures. However, clawback provisions require GPs to return any previously received carry if, at the final liquidation of the fund, total LP returns fall short of the committed capital plus preferred return. Because clawback is generally enforceable, the total amount of carry the GP ultimately keeps is the same under both models if the fund is successful — the difference is purely timing. The practical implication is that GPs who benefit from deal-by-deal distributions have a stronger incentive to manage later investments aggressively to avoid triggering clawbacks.

## 84. LBO-Modeling-Working-Through-the-Dell-LBO-on-a-Cocktail-Napkin-Part-1

This video introduces a "cocktail napkin" LBO analysis using the Dell take-private (2013) as the example — a back-of-envelope framework for quickly estimating IRR without a full three-statement model. The Dell LBO: offer price of $13.88/share × 1.69 billion diluted shares = $23.5B equity purchase price; plus refinancing of $1.4B legacy debt = $24.9B total uses. Funding sources: $11.5B in new debt + $7.7B of balance-sheet cash used = $19.2B, implying $5.7B in new sponsor equity. LTM EBITDA was $3.5 billion (down ~20% year-over-year), implying an entry EV/EBITDA multiple of approximately 4.9× (EV = $23.5B equity + $1.4B debt − $7.7B cash = $17.2B; $17.2B / $3.5B = 4.9×). The base-case exit assumptions: same EBITDA at $3.5B five years later, same 4.9× multiple (conservative, same entry-exit), full debt paydown, no cash retained. Under these assumptions, exit equity value = exit enterprise value = $17.2B (with zero net debt); IRR = approximately 24% over five years on $5.7B invested. The video emphasizes that only two assumptions truly drive LBO returns on a napkin: exit EBITDA and exit multiple — which is why investment committees focus so intensely on the realism of these two inputs.

## 85. LBO-Modeling-Working-Through-the-Dell-LBO-on-a-Cocktail-Napkin-Part-2

This video works through the Dell cocktail-napkin solution step by step, confirming the setup from Part 1 and extending the analysis. The equity purchase price of $23.5B (1.69B × $13.88) plus $1.4B legacy debt refinancing totals $24.9B in uses; against $11.5B in new debt and $7.7B in balance-sheet cash, the residual sponsor equity is $5.7B.
